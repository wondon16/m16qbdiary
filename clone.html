Film Study Methods

  • Dont just watch highlights — watch the entire game flow. This helps you pick up on rhythm, fatigue patterns, substitution strategies, and how teams adjust throughout quarters/halves. Pay attention to the “small things” between plays: how players react after mistakes, how the coach signals in plays, or if tempo changes after certain situations (e.g., turnovers, timeouts, foul trouble).
  • Instead of following the ball every time, lock in on your position or the one you’ll be matched against. Notice:
    • Stance and alignment: Are they balanced? Do they tip off run/pass/shot direction?
    • Footwork: Is it explosive or lazy at certain points?
    • Conditioning: Do they gas out after long drives or late in games? By isolating positions, you catch tendencies that the broadcast angle misses.
  • Zoom in on the top threats or your likely matchup. Ask:
    • Do they favor going left or right?
    • Do they always spin a certain way?
    • How do they react to pressure vs. space?
    • What’s their “go-to move” when things break down? If you learn a player’s comfort zone, you can force them away from it.
  • Find their weakness!? Everyone has one — it might be physical, technical, or mental.
    • Physical: slow first step, lack of strength, poor conditioning.
    • Technical: sloppy ball handling, weak hand/foot, struggles against zone/press.
    • Mental: frustration after mistakes, slow to adjust, rattled by noise/pressure. Spot these cracks early, because good teams exploit them.
  • Formations, alignments, and spacing usually signal intent. Track how often a certain set leads to a type of play. For example:
    • Trips formation = screen game or quick slant.
    • 5-out = drive-and-kick action.
    • Wing overloaded = iso or pick-and-roll coming. Build a mental chart: “When they line up like this, 70% of the time they run X.”
  • Combine all the above:
    • Pick plays that force their weak hand/foot.
    • Target their tired players late in the game.
    • Design mismatches through switches or screens. The key isn’t just knowing their weakness, but knowing how to repeatedly stress it until it breaks.

Category 3

Fuel & Hydration

Pre-Game Fuel — Night Before Meal

1. Grilled Chicken, Brown Rice & Steamed Broccoli

Nutrients:
Lean protein (chicken) → muscle repair & recovery
Complex carbs (brown rice) → steady energy for game day
Fiber + vitamins (broccoli) → digestion & immune support

2. Baked Salmon, Roasted Sweet Potatoes & Spinach Salad

Nutrients:
Omega-3 fats (salmon) → reduce inflammation, improve focus
Complex carbs (sweet potatoes) → slow-release fuel
Iron + nitrates (spinach) → boosts blood flow & energy

3. Lean Turkey, Quinoa & Roasted Veggies (peppers/onions)

Nutrients:
Lean protein (turkey) → keeps muscles stocked with amino acids
Complete protein + carbs (quinoa) → sustained energy & recovery
Antioxidants (peppers/onions) → fights inflammation + supports joints

Tips:
Keep seasoning simple: olive oil, sea salt, black pepper, fresh herbs
Avoid heavy creams, fried foods, and late-night desserts (slows digestion)
Drink water with dinner (not soda or sugary drinks)

Game-Day Breakfast

1. Bowl: Oatmeal, Banana, Peanut Butter & Cinnamon

Why it works:
Oatmeal → slow-burning carbs
Banana → potassium (prevents cramps)
Peanut butter → healthy fats + protein
Cinnamon → keeps blood sugar stable

2. Eggs, Whole Grain Toast & Berries

Why it works:
Eggs → high-quality protein for muscles
Toast → carbs for quick energy
Berries → antioxidants to reduce inflammation

3. Greek Yogurt Parfait (Yogurt, Granola, Honey, Fruit)

Why it works:
Greek yogurt → protein + probiotics (gut health)
Granola → fast + slow carbs
Honey → quick energy boost
Fruit → vitamins + hydration

Timing Tips:
Eat breakfast 3–4 hours before kickoff
Hydrate with water or electrolyte drink alongside breakfast
Add a light snack 60–90 min before game (banana, granola bar, rice cake + nut butter)

Hydration Plan (before/during game & post-game)

Baseline:
Start hydrating the day before (½ your bodyweight in ounces of water).
Limit soda, sugary drinks, and nothing heavy at night. Keep a water bottle in sight all day.

Night Before the Game
Drink 16–20 oz of water with dinner.
Sip water steadily throughout the evening (don’t chug all at once).
Option: add an electrolyte packet or pinch of sea salt + squeeze of lemon if you’ve been sweating a lot that week.
⚡ Goal: Go to bed hydrated, not bloated.

Morning of the Game
Drink 16 oz of water right after waking up.
With breakfast, sip another 8–12 oz of water or electrolyte drink.
⚡ Goal: Replace overnight loss + start fueling cells early.

2 Hours Before Kickoff
Drink 16–20 oz of water.
If it’s hot: include electrolytes to prevent cramping.
⚡ Goal: Top off hydration tanks without feeling heavy.

During the Game
Every quarter: sip 3–6 oz of water or sports drink.
Rotate: water for thirst, electrolytes for endurance.
⚡ Goal: Stay consistent, never wait until you feel thirsty.

Post-Game
Drink 16–24 oz of water for every pound lost (weigh before & after if possible).
Add electrolytes if sweating was heavy.
⚡ Goal: Rehydrate fast to recover faster.

Category 4

Mental Focus & Throwing

Pre-Game Mental Focus

Breathing Technique (describe or choose):

  • 4-4-4 Box Breath — Inhale 4s → Hold 4s → Exhale 4s (Repeat 4–6 cycles)
  • 6-2-6 Calm Breath — Inhale 6s → Hold 2s → Exhale 6s (Repeat until relaxed)
  • Visualization Breath — Inhale = picture the snap. Exhale = picture completing the throw. Repeat with different plays in your mind.

Throwing Warm-Up Routine

Pre-game throwing progression: 7 throws each

  • Basketball shots
  • Right leg down left leg is kicking and twisting
  • Left leg down right leg is twisting and kicking
  • Side ways (right leg in front) twisting torso throwing over hip
  • Left leg in front throwing over outside hip twisting torso
  • 3 step drop spot throws
  • 3 and hitch spot throws
  • 5 step (if needed) spot throws
  • Throw on the run (spot throws)
  • Touch passes (spot throws: something over the shoulder/ i.e. fades or corners)
  • ☑ Routes on air with WRs

Category 5

Coverage Recognition Help

Coverage Shell Quick List

  • Cover 3 Zone
  • Cover 2 Zone
  • Quarters
  • Cover 1 Free Man
  • Cover 0 Man

Pre-Snap Checklist

  • Always get to the line quickly with your hands under center.
    • If the defense is going to shift, they will do it after you get under center.
  • Look for the safeties.
    • Is there anyone in the middle of the field?
  • Look for the best side to read.
    • Number of defenders vs. number of receivers.
  • Look at the defender’s leverage.
    • Leverage refers to the defender’s relationship to the receiver, or the area of the field the defender must cover.
  • Know where all the routes are going to be run.
    • Look for possible blitzes.
    • Know where your hot routes are.
  • At this point the QB will have a pretty good idea of where he is going to go with the ball — this is extremely important.

Dropback Reminders

  • Realize you are making the final decision on where to go with the ball on your way back — not at the end of your drop.
    • If you haven’t made a decision by the time you get to the end of your drop, go to the contingency plan right away.
  • With the exception of a quick 3-step drop, take two shuffle-steps up in the pocket max before going to the contingency plan.

Ten Basic Reads (Top Gun)

  1. #1 The Slant Read
    • Key flat defender — OLB, strong safety, or corner.
    • Throw away from the flat defender; if he covers the slant, hit the flat or swing route.
    • If he goes flat, hit the slant.
    • The drop matches his leverage: three-big if he is close to the line, three-quick if he is playing off.
  2. #2 The Stick Read
    • Key the strong safety or defender who will cover that area.
    • Check pre-snap leverage.
    • Look for the flat, then the stick.
    • Looking to the flat first often opens the stick route.
    • Versus Cover 2, look to the strong-side fade first.
  3. #3 The Inside Sideline Read
    • Key the strong safety or defender responsible for that area.
    • Check pre-snap leverage.
    • Look for the swing, then the sideline.
    • Looking at the swing route first often opens the sideline route.
  4. #4 The Curl Read
    • Key flat defender.
    • Throw away from the flat defender.
    • If he goes flat, hit the curl.
    • If he drops to the curl, hit the flat or swing route.
  5. #5 The Sideline Read
    • Versus Cover 2, the sideline will automatically turn into a fade route.
    • Check corner and safety alignment.
    • Check OLB or strong safety alignment.
    • Key flat defender.
    • If the flat defender can get underneath the route, throw to the stop or seam.
  6. #6 The Dig Read
    • Key the underneath coverage.
    • While dropping back, take a look at what the linebackers are doing.
    • If they drop, hit the drag.
    • If they jump or wall the drag, go for the dig.
    • Versus Quarters coverage, the drag usually becomes the safest throw.
  7. #7 The Double Square-in Read
    • Key outside linebacker to the strong safety.
    • If the linebacker takes the medium square-in and the strong safety takes the flat, hit the deep square-in.
    • If either defender takes away the deep square-in, throw to the area they vacate.
  8. #8 The Deep Out Read
    • Great route versus Cover 2 — the flat route will hold the corner.
    • If the corner runs with the wide receiver, hit the flat route immediately.
    • If the corner comes off the wide receiver, continue your drop and hit the deep out.
  9. #9 The Deep Reads
    • This is a timing pass.
    • Realize that deep passes are not high percentage completions.
    • Get a good idea where you have the best chance to go with the ball on your pre-snap read.
    • If you are not sure where to go deep with the ball on your third step of a five-step drop, get the ball to an underneath receiver.
    • Four Verticals vs. Cover Three:
      • The inside seams are the first read.
      • Hit the seams as they pass the linebackers at about 15 yards.
      • Look the safety off with a good look at the wide receivers as you drop.
      • Throw away from safety coverage.
  10. #10 Man (Single-Coverage) Leverage Reads
    • Where is the blitz coming from?
      • Identify your hot route(s).
    • What are the receiver routes?
      • Think about possible safety help.
    • What are the individual match-ups?
      • Does a particular receiver have an advantage against his defender?
    • Which receiver has leverage on his defender?
      • Which defender is in a bad position to cover the called route?

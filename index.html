    Ways I studied film this week:

    • 1. Watch the Game Fully With No Interruptions Dont just watch highlights ‚Äî watch the entire game flow. This helps you pick up on rhythm, fatigue patterns, substitution strategies, and how teams adjust throughout quarters/halves. Pay attention to the ‚Äúsmall things‚Äù between plays: how players react after mistakes, how the coach signals in plays, or if tempo changes after certain situations (e.g., turnovers, timeouts, foul trouble).
    • 2. Watch Specific Positions Instead of following the ball every time, lock in on your position or the one you‚Äôll be matched against. Notice: Stance and alignment: Are they balanced? Do they tip off run/pass/shot direction? Footwork: Is it explosive or lazy at certain points? Conditioning: Do they gas out after long drives or late in games?     • 3. Key on Certain Player Tendencies Zoom in on the top threats or your likely matchup. Ask: Do they favor going left or right? Do they always spin a certain way? How do they react to pressure vs. space? Whats their ‚Äúgo-to move‚Äù when things break down? If you learn a players comfort zone, you can force them away from it.
    • 4. Find Their Weaknesses Everyone has one ‚Äî it might be physical, technical, or mental. Physical: slow first step, lack of strength, poor conditioning. Spot these cracks early, because good teams exploit them.
    • Study Formations to Spot Tendencies Formations, alignments, and spacing usually signal intent. Track how often a certain set leads to a type of play.       • 7. Figure Out How You Can Attack Their Weaknesses Combine all the above: Pick plays that force their weak hand/foot. Target their tired players late in the game.
